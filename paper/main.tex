\begin{abstract}
Vision-language models (VLMs) are being rapidly deployed in
autonomous-driving stacks for high-level scene understanding, yet
their reliability as \emph{anomaly detectors} for safety-critical
deployment remains poorly characterised. We introduce \bench, a
benchmark of \textbf{15{,}000} dashcam images comprising
\textbf{10{,}000} normal driving scenes and \textbf{5{,}000} anomalous
scenes spanning \textbf{11} hazard categories with rich annotations:
anomaly class, free-form description, severity, ego-vehicle risk
level, and recommended driving action. Using \bench, we conduct an
extensive empirical study of \textbf{nine} state-of-the-art open
VLMs (InternVL3-1B/2B/8B, Qwen2-VL-2B, Qwen2.5-VL-3B/7B,
LLaVA-OneVision-7B, LLaVA-1.6-13B, LLaMA-3.2-11B-Vision) and
\textbf{seven} baselines (three CLIP and three SigLIP variants and a
reconstruction autoencoder) across three tasks: binary anomaly
detection, multiclass classification, and free-form anomaly
description. We further analyse the models along five dimensions:
(i)~per-class difficulty, (ii)~confidence calibration,
(iii)~cross-model agreement and ensemble ceilings,
(iv)~vision-encoder and language-model representational geometry, and
(v)~prediction consistency under stochastic decoding. Our findings
reveal counter-intuitive results: a 3B-parameter VLM
(Qwen2.5-VL-3B) is the best binary detector and best calibrated
model, despite far larger competitors performing worse;
representations from the InternVL3 family are near-identical across
the 1B--8B scale, suggesting scale alone fails to diversify learned
features; and self-reported confidence scores from most VLMs are
poorly calibrated, with severe over- and under-confidence patterns
that pose direct safety hazards. \bench, all code, and all
predictions are released under a permissive license to enable rapid
follow-up research.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Autonomous-driving (AD) systems must reliably detect and reason about
\emph{any} hazard a vehicle could encounter, including long-tailed
events absent from training distributions: animals on the road, fallen
debris, unusual vehicles, infrastructure failures, and adverse-lighting
conditions. Recent vision-language models (VLMs)~\citep{internvl3, qwen25vl,
llava_onevision} have shown impressive open-vocabulary scene
understanding, leading several proposals to put VLMs in the AD
perception stack as semantic supervisors~\citep{semantic_observer,
scvlm} or end-to-end planners~\citep{simlingo, autovla}. Yet
the central question---\emph{are VLMs reliable enough as anomaly
detectors for safety-critical deployment?}---is poorly answered by
existing benchmarks, which either focus on visual-question
answering~\citep{drivelm, nuscenes_qa, nuscenes_mqa},
trustworthiness~\citep{autotrust}, or robustness to corrupted
inputs~\citep{drivebench}, but seldom on the specific task of
detecting and characterising anomalous scenes.

Existing AD anomaly benchmarks tend to focus on
segmentation~\citep{ood_seg_survey} or specific narrow categories such
as collision prediction or corner cases~\citep{coda, tudat,
twocoool}. They lack the breadth (multiple hazard classes), the depth
(rich annotations beyond a single label), and the model-side
diversity (modern open-source VLMs of varying scale and family) needed
to draw \emph{methodological} conclusions about VLM
reliability. Without such conclusions, deployment decisions are made on
ad-hoc, often closed-source, evaluations.

\textbf{Our contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item We introduce \bench, a 15{,}000-image dashcam benchmark with
        five-attribute annotations on the 5{,}000 anomalous
        images (anomaly class, free-form description, key facts,
        severity, recommended action). Anomalous-vs-normal labels are
        human-curated; classes and descriptions are auto-generated by
        GPT-4o and cross-validated by Claude Sonnet~4.6.
  \item We propose a three-task evaluation protocol covering
        \emph{binary detection}, \emph{multiclass classification}, and
        \emph{free-form description}, with shared inputs and
        complementary metric suites.
  \item We benchmark 9 open-source VLMs and 7 visual-similarity /
        reconstruction baselines, totalling 16 models across the three
        tasks. We additionally measure per-image inference latency on
        a single RTX 5090.
  \item We provide a five-axis analysis of VLM behaviour:
        per-class difficulty, calibration (ECE), pairwise agreement,
        representation geometry (UMAP, CKA, layer probes), and
        consistency under stochastic decoding.
  \item Our findings expose three deployment-relevant pathologies:
        (a) parameter count is a poor predictor of detection
        performance; (b) self-reported confidence is poorly calibrated
        in 7 of 9 VLMs, with multiple cases of severe over- or
        under-confidence; (c) within-family scaling produces
        near-identical representations (CKA $>$ 0.93 across InternVL3
        scales), so ensembling within a family yields little benefit.
\end{itemize}

The benchmark, all model predictions, every saved hidden-state
representation, and the full evaluation code will be released under
a CC-BY 4.0 license at the project URL upon acceptance.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{VLM benchmarks for autonomous driving.}
DriveLM~\citep{drivelm} and NuScenes-QA~\citep{nuscenes_qa} pioneered
graph- and pair-style VQA over the nuScenes dataset.
NuScenes-MQA~\citep{nuscenes_mqa} added integrated caption-and-QA
evaluation. AutoTrust~\citep{autotrust} probes trustworthiness across
six VLMs along five axes (trustfulness, safety, robustness, privacy,
fairness), finding that generalist VLMs surprisingly outperform
driving-specialised ones. DriveBench~\citep{drivebench} evaluates 12
VLMs across 17 settings (clean, corrupted, text-only) and shows that
VLMs often respond from textual cues rather than visual
grounding. AutoVLA~\citep{autovla} and SimLingo~\citep{simlingo} push
toward end-to-end vision-language-action driving but evaluate primarily
on planning, not detection. Compared with these works, \bench focuses
explicitly on the \emph{anomaly-detection} use-case, with a curated
class taxonomy that is absent from existing benchmarks.

\paragraph{Anomaly detection in autonomous driving.}
Out-of-distribution segmentation has been the dominant framing
\citep{ood_seg_survey}, with corner-case datasets such as
CODA~\citep{coda} and TU-DAT~\citep{tudat} for object-level evaluation
and reconstruction-based methods such as the hybrid memory autoencoder
of \citet{hybrid_vad} for video-level detection.
ScVLM~\citep{scvlm} fine-tunes VLMs for safety-critical event
classification. The 2COOOL workshop~\citep{twocoool} explicitly targets
out-of-label hazard understanding. Our benchmark complements these by
providing a unified scene-level evaluation in image space across
multiple semantic axes simultaneously.

\paragraph{General VLM evaluation, hallucination, calibration.}
MME~\citep{mme} introduced a broad MLLM benchmark covering 14
sub-tasks. DASH~\citep{dash} characterises systematic VLM
hallucinations. Trust-but-Verify~\citep{trust_but_verify} proposes
programmatic VLM verification. On the calibration axis,
VL-Calibration~\citep{vl_calibration} reduces ECE on Qwen3-VL from 0.42
to 0.10, and decision-aware uncertainty
evaluation~\citep{decision_aware_unc} applies conformal prediction to
20 VLMs. ECE itself is well-studied~\citep{ece_understanding}. Our
work measures calibration across 9 driving-specific VLMs, complementing
these general-domain studies.

\paragraph{Representation analysis.}
Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)~\citep{cka_kornblith} is the de-facto
metric for cross-model representation similarity, with known
limitations~\citep{cka_reliability}. Layer-wise representation
progression has been characterised in transformers~\citep{layerwise_sim}
and linear probing of LLM hidden states is increasingly used to assess
implicit knowledge encoding~\citep{linear_probe_neurips}. We adopt
both metrics for our cross-model and within-model representational
analysis.

\paragraph{LLM-as-judge for dataset validation.}
JudgeBench~\citep{judgebench} and \citet{llm_judge_survey}
characterise the reliability of LLM-as-judge evaluations. We follow
their guidance and use Claude Sonnet 4.6 as a cross-validating judge
for the GPT-4o-generated annotations on \bench.

\section{The \bench Benchmark}
\label{sec:dataset}

\input{tables/01_dataset_stats}

\bench contains 15{,}000 dashcam images split into 10{,}000 normal
driving scenes and 5{,}000 anomalous scenes covering 11 hazard
categories. Per-class counts are intentionally long-tailed
(Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats}), reflecting real-world frequency:
oversized vehicles (n=2{,}037) and human-presence anomalies (n=728)
are common; multi-hazard compound scenes (n=33) are rare.

\paragraph{Image collection and curation.}
We aggregated dashcam frames from a mixture of publicly licensed
autonomous-driving datasets and our own curation pipeline, with
manual screening to ensure (i) image quality is sufficient for visual
reasoning, (ii) no personally identifiable information (faces, license
plates) is recoverable, and (iii) the
normal/anomalous label is correct. The binary anomalous-vs-normal
label is \emph{human-curated}: an author manually inspected every
anomalous candidate before inclusion.

\paragraph{Rich annotations on the anomalous subset.}
Each of the 5{,}000 anomalous images is annotated with seven
attributes:
\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item \texttt{anomaly\_class} --- one of the 11 hazard categories.
  \item \texttt{anomaly\_description} --- a free-form natural language
        description (mean 35 words).
  \item \texttt{anomaly\_key\_facts} --- a list of 3--5 atomic visual
        observations.
  \item \texttt{severity} $\in$ \{\textit{minor}, \textit{moderate},
        \textit{severe}, \textit{critical}\}.
  \item \texttt{ego\_risk\_level} $\in$ \{\textit{none},
        \textit{caution}, \textit{warning}, \textit{danger}\}.
  \item \texttt{recommended\_action} --- one of seven driving actions
        (e.g., \textit{reduce\_speed}, \textit{change\_lane}).
  \item \texttt{benchmark\_difficulty} $\in$ \{\textit{easy},
        \textit{medium}, \textit{hard}\}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Annotation pipeline and validation.}
Class and description annotations were generated by GPT-4o with a
structured prompt enforcing the 11-class taxonomy. To validate these
auto-generated labels, we re-annotated all 5{,}000 anomalous images
with Claude Sonnet 4.6, asking it to (i) independently classify the
primary anomaly without seeing GPT-4o's label and (ii) rate the
GPT-4o-generated description on factuality (1--5) and completeness
(1--5). Inter-annotator class agreement is reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:validation}; we manually reviewed every disagreement
to produce the final gold labels. Disagreements were resolved by an
author looking at the actual image.

\section{Tasks and Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:tasks}

\bench supports three complementary tasks. The model receives the
image and a structured natural-language prompt; output formats are
constrained to JSON for reliable parsing.

\paragraph{Task 1 -- Binary anomaly detection (15{,}000 images).}
Predict \texttt{anomaly\_present} $\in \{\text{true},
\text{false}\}$ together with a confidence score $c \in [0,1]$ and a
brief reasoning string. Metrics: accuracy, balanced accuracy,
precision, recall, F1, MCC, AUROC, AUPRC.

\paragraph{Task 2 -- Multiclass classification (5{,}000 anomalous images).}
Given an image already known to be anomalous, predict the
\texttt{anomaly\_class} from the 11-class taxonomy, plus a confidence
score and reasoning string. Metrics: accuracy, balanced accuracy,
macro/weighted F1, macro precision, macro recall, Cohen's $\kappa$,
plus the full per-class confusion matrix.

\paragraph{Task 3 -- Free-form description (5{,}000 anomalous images).}
Generate a natural-language description of the anomaly. Metrics:
BLEU-1, BLEU-4, METEOR, ROUGE-L, BERTScore (P/R/F1) against the
GPT-4o reference description; we also report mean inference latency
in seconds, since description-task latency dominates real-time
deployment cost.

The exact prompts used for each task are reproduced in
Appendix~\ref{app:prompts}.

\section{Evaluated Models}
\label{sec:models}

We evaluate three families of methods:

\paragraph{Vision-language models (9).}
InternVL3 family at three scales (1B, 2B, 8B)~\citep{internvl3};
Qwen2-VL-2B and Qwen2.5-VL-3B/7B~\citep{qwen25vl}; LLaVA-OneVision-7B
and LLaVA-1.6-13B~\citep{llava_onevision, llava_15};
LLaMA-3.2-11B-Vision~\citep{llama32_vision}. All are evaluated
zero-shot, queried with the prompts in
Appendix~\ref{app:prompts}.

\paragraph{Visual-similarity baselines (6).}
Three CLIP variants (ViT-B/32, ViT-B/16, ViT-L/14)~\citep{clip} and
three SigLIP variants (Base/16-224, Large/16-256,
SO400m/14-384)~\citep{siglip2}. For binary, we use the standard
two-prompt softmax scoring (``a normal driving scene'' vs.\ ``an
anomalous driving scene''). For multiclass, we score each image
against 11 natural-language class descriptions and take
\textit{argmax}.

\paragraph{Reconstruction baseline (1).}
A convolutional autoencoder trained on the 10{,}000 normal images,
using reconstruction MSE as anomaly score. It serves as a classical
unsupervised baseline.

\paragraph{Findings.}
(1) Qwen2.5-VL-3B is the dominant binary detector
(Acc=0.92, MCC=0.82, AUPRC=0.82), beating models with $4\times$ more
parameters. (2) Several VLMs (InternVL3-1B,
Qwen2-VL-2B) collapse to predicting ``anomaly always''
(precision $\to$ 1.0 with recall $\to$ 0), a known prompt-following
failure mode in smaller VLMs. (3) The
autoencoder underperforms strongly (AUROC=0.22, AUPRC=0.22):
reconstruction error is not a useful anomaly signal in this domain
because anomalous images often have similar pixel statistics to
normal ones (different objects but similar textures). (4) CLIP variants are surprisingly competitive in
AUROC ($\sim$0.80) but suffer in precision; SigLIP variants exhibit
the opposite trade-off.

\subsection{Multiclass Anomaly Classification}
\label{sec:multiclass}

\input{tables/03_multiclass_results}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/per_class_f1_heatmap.pdf}

Two VLM families dominate multiclass: \textbf{InternVL3-8B}
(macro-F1 = 0.53, accuracy = 0.76) and \textbf{Qwen2.5-VL-7B}
(macro-F1 = 0.47, accuracy = 0.72). Smaller VLMs (1--3B) collapse on

CLIP/SigLIP zero-shot baselines achieve weak but non-trivial macro F1

visual signal, but lack the structured reasoning that VLMs leverage.

\paragraph{The detection-vs-classification dissociation.}
Notably, the best binary detector (Qwen2.5-VL-3B, Acc=0.92) is
\emph{not} the best multiclass classifier (rank 5 of 9 VLMs). This
dissociation suggests that detecting ``something is wrong'' relies on
different reasoning than identifying ``what is wrong''.
Figure~\ref{fig:det_cls} (Appendix~\ref{app:appfigs}) visualises this
dissociation across the 9 VLMs.

\subsection{Free-Form Anomaly Description}
\label{sec:description}

\input{tables/04_description_results}

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Per-Class Difficulty}
\label{sec:class_difficulty}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/hardest_classes.pdf}
  \caption{Mean F1 across 9 VLMs per anomaly class, with error bars
           showing standard deviation. Class support shown on the
           right. Strange-Object-on-Road, Adverse Lighting, and
           Multi-Hazard are universally hard
           ($\bar{\text{F1}} < 0.05$); Animal-on-Road and Oversized
           Vehicle are universally easier
           ($\bar{\text{F1}} > 0.40$).}
  \label{fig:hardest_classes}
\end{figure}

The class-wise F1 distribution
(Fig.~\ref{fig:hardest_classes}) reveals a clean dichotomy. Classes
with distinctive visual signatures and likely high pre-training
exposure (animals, large vehicles) achieve mean F1 $>$ 0.40 across
models. Classes that are visually subtle or context-dependent
(strange-object, adverse-lighting, multi-hazard) have mean F1 $<$
0.05---essentially failure across the model zoo. This is
deployment-critical: the universally hard classes are also among the
\emph{most safety-critical} (debris on the road can cause crashes,
adverse lighting is a leading cause of accidents). VLMs are not
ready for these cases.

\subsection{Confidence Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/reliability_diagrams.pdf}
  \caption{Reliability diagrams for the binary task. Each subplot
           shows accuracy versus self-reported confidence; green bars =
           underconfident, red bars = overconfident. ECE is annotated
           in each panel.}
  \label{fig:reliability}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/calibration_ece.pdf}
  \caption{Calibration error (ECE, lower is better) and overconfidence
           score (positive = overconfident) per model.}
  \label{fig:ece}
\end{figure}

Confidence calibration is critical for safety-critical deployment:
when a VLM reports 0.95 confidence in a hazard, downstream planners
need that number to mean what it says. \textbf{Only Qwen2.5-VL-3B is
well-calibrated} (ECE=0.027). Two models exhibit \emph{severe}
overconfidence (InternVL3-1B: ECE=0.617, mean confidence 0.95 with
accuracy 0.33; LLaVA-1.6-13B: ECE=0.227). Two models exhibit
\emph{severe underconfidence} (Qwen2.5-VL-7B and LLaMA-3.2-11B both
report mean confidence $\sim$0.25 despite achieving accuracy $>$0.78).
Underconfidence is equally dangerous in deployment: planners would
ignore correctly-flagged hazards.

The pairwise agreement matrix (Fig.~\ref{fig:agree_matrix}) reveals

(agreement $<$ 0.65 with all others). This has direct implications

single model (0.91); but adding more models from related families
plateaus the ensemble curve. \textbf{Diversity, not scale, drives
ensemble gains.}

\subsection{Representational Geometry}
\label{sec:representations}

We saved per-image hidden-state representations during inference for
every (model, task, image) triple, totalling $\sim$135K representation
files. Each contains the per-layer mean-pooled vision-encoder
hidden states and the projected vision embedding the LLM ingests.
We use this to ask three questions:

\paragraph{(a) Do vision encoders separate anomalous from normal?}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/vision_umap_correct_wrong_binary.pdf}
  \caption{PCA of vision-encoder representations of 600 images
           (300 normal + 300 anomalous), per model. Green = correct
           binary prediction, red = wrong.}
  \label{fig:umap_binary}
\end{figure}

For 6 of the 9 VLMs, the vision encoder
already separates correct from wrong predictions in 2D PCA
(Fig.~\ref{fig:umap_binary}). For Qwen2.5-VL-3B, this separation is
particularly crisp---wrong predictions form a small, dense red
cluster, while correct ones occupy the rest of the space. This
suggests the representations themselves contain the binary signal,
even when the LLM's textual output gets it wrong.

\paragraph{(b) How similar are different VLMs' representations?}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/cka_matrix.pdf}
  \caption{Linear CKA between models on 300 anomalous images. Numbers
           in cells are the actual CKA values.}
  \label{fig:cka}
\end{figure}

The CKA matrix (Fig.~\ref{fig:cka}) confirms that
\textbf{within-family scaling does not diversify representations}:
InternVL3-1B vs InternVL3-8B has CKA = 0.93 despite the $8\times$
parameter difference. The most architecturally distinct model is
LLaMA-3.2-11B (CKA $\sim$0.25--0.30 with all other models), which
uses a cross-attention vision design rather than the early-fusion
projection used by the others. This finding suggests that combining
\emph{architecturally diverse} VLMs in an ensemble is more
beneficial than combining \emph{scale-diverse} VLMs of the same
family.

\paragraph{(c) Where in the network is anomaly information encoded?}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{subfigure}{0.49\linewidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/linear_probe_overlay.pdf}
    \caption{Linear-probe accuracy per layer.}
    \label{fig:probe}
  \end{subfigure}
  \begin{subfigure}{0.49\linewidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/layer_drift_overlay.pdf}
    \caption{Layer-wise representation drift.}
    \label{fig:drift}
  \end{subfigure}
  \caption{Layer-wise analysis of vision-encoder representations.
           Probes trained at each layer; drift is cosine
           distance between consecutive layers.}
  \label{fig:layer_combined}
\end{figure}

Linear probes (Fig.~\ref{fig:probe}) trained on each layer's
representations to predict the 11-class label show monotonic
improvement with depth across all 9 VLMs, peaking at the final layer
($\bar{\text{Bal.~Acc.}} \approx 0.40$). Final-layer probe accuracy
\emph{exceeds} most VLMs' zero-shot accuracy on the multiclass
task---suggesting that the visual features already contain the
answer, but the language model fails to extract it via prompting.
\textbf{This motivates a high-impact follow-up: train a small
classification head on frozen vision-encoder features.} Our linear
probes already provide an upper bound on what such a model could
achieve. The
layer-drift plot (Fig.~\ref{fig:drift}) shows the largest
representation changes happen in the early layers (drift $>$ 0.4) and
plateau by layer 12, which is consistent with most class-discriminative
information being formed in the middle of the network.

\subsection{Vision-Encoder Class Geometry}
\label{sec:class_geometry}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/vision_umap_by_class.pdf}
  \caption{PCA of vision-encoder representations of 600 anomalous
           images coloured by anomaly class.}
  \label{fig:umap_class}
\end{figure}

Beyond binary correctness, we ask whether the 11 classes form
visually-coherent clusters in the vision encoder's output space
(Fig.~\ref{fig:umap_class}). Even for our best multiclass model
(InternVL3-8B), classes do not separate cleanly in 2D
PCA. This indicates that the vision encoder is not strongly
class-discriminative; the LLM does the bulk of the multiclass
reasoning via cross-attention to the projected vision embedding. A
contrastive vision-encoder objective targeted at anomaly classes
could meaningfully reshape this geometry.

\subsection{Consistency under Stochastic Decoding}
\label{sec:consistency}

We measure prediction consistency under temperature-$0.7$ decoding
with $N{=}5$ runs per image on \emph{both} tasks. We report
majority-vote (MV) accuracy as well as mean consistency (fraction of
runs agreeing with the MV) and predictive entropy across runs.

\paragraph{Binary task.}
\input{tables/08_consistency_binary}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/binary_consistency_overview.pdf}
  \caption{Cross-model comparison of binary-detection consistency under
           stochastic decoding ($T{=}0.7$, $N{=}5$ runs).
           Top-left: majority-vote balanced accuracy.
           Top-right: mean consistency (fraction of runs agreeing
           with the majority).
           Bottom-left: mean entropy (lower = more consistent).
           Bottom-right: ECE (lower = better calibrated).}
  \label{fig:binary_consistency}
\end{figure}

Table~\ref{tab:consistency_binary} and
Fig.~\ref{fig:binary_consistency} report consistency on the binary
task. \textbf{Qwen2.5-VL-3B retains its dominance under sampling} —
majority-vote balanced accuracy = 0.91 with mean consistency $>$ 0.97
and ECE = 0.024, indicating that its single-shot binary
predictions reflect a stable underlying decision. In contrast,
InternVL3-1B's high consistency (0.83) is misleading: it stems from
\emph{consistently predicting ``anomaly''} regardless of input,
yielding ECE = 0.66. This decouples \emph{consistency} from
\emph{correctness}: a model can be very consistent and very wrong.

\paragraph{Multiclass task.}
\input{tables/06_consistency_multiclass}

The strongest models on multiclass (InternVL3-8B, Qwen2.5-VL-7B) are
also the most consistent (mean consistency $>$ 0.85), suggesting
they are confident not just in a single sampled answer but in the
underlying class assignment. Several weaker models exhibit high
entropy across runs, meaning their multiclass predictions are
essentially noise.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Implications for VLM-based AD systems.}
Our results have three deployment-relevant implications. First,
\textbf{parameter count is a weak predictor of detection performance};
practitioners should benchmark on their target task, not pick the
largest available model. Second, \textbf{self-reported confidence is
unreliable in the majority of VLMs}; downstream safety-critical
planners must apply post-hoc calibration~\citep{vl_calibration} before
using these scores. Third, \textbf{representation analysis suggests an
underexploited capability}: linear probes on frozen features already
beat zero-shot prompting on multiclass classification, with much
lower latency.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1) Class and description annotations were generated by GPT-4o; we
mitigated this with cross-LLM validation by Claude Sonnet 4.6 and
human review of disagreements (Appendix~\ref{app:validation}), but
some long-tail biases of GPT-4o may persist. (2) The benchmark is
single-image; temporal anomalies (e.g., gradual lane drift) are out of
scope. (3) Our model selection focuses on open-source VLMs; we did
not evaluate proprietary models (GPT-4V, Gemini, Claude) on the full
benchmark due to API cost. (4) The absolute accuracy numbers reflect
zero-shot prompting; fine-tuning on the training portion of \bench
would likely improve all models substantially---this is left to
follow-up work.

\paragraph{Negative societal impacts.}
Releasing a benchmark for VLM-based hazard detection in autonomous
driving could be misinterpreted as endorsing the deployment of
current VLMs in safety-critical contexts, which our findings argue
\emph{against}. We explicitly warn in the dataset documentation that
no model in our zoo achieves accuracy sufficient for unattended AD
deployment on the long-tailed classes. Conversely, the benchmark
should accelerate the development of safer VLM-based perception
modules.

% =============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced \bench, a 15{,}000-image dashcam benchmark for
VLM-based anomaly detection in autonomous driving with rich
annotations across three tasks. Our extensive empirical study of 16
models (9 VLMs + 6 visual-similarity baselines + 1 reconstruction
autoencoder) reveals deployment-critical pathologies in current
state-of-the-art VLMs: weak-to-poor calibration, family-level
representation collinearity, and surprising scale-vs-performance
inversions. We hope \bench enables rigorous follow-up research on
the reliability of VLM-based AD perception.

\section{Annotation Pipeline and Cross-LLM Validation}
\label{app:validation}

\paragraph{Annotation pipeline.}
Each anomalous image is processed through a structured GPT-4o prompt
that enforces (i) the 11-class taxonomy with detailed disambiguation
rules, (ii) JSON-schema output, and (iii) consistency checks across
the 7 attributes (e.g.,
\texttt{ego\_risk\_level=\textit{none}} requires
\texttt{anomaly\_present=\textit{false}}). The prompt is reproduced
in Appendix~\ref{app:annotation_prompt}.

\paragraph{Validation methodology.}
Following recommendations from the LLM-as-judge
literature~\citep{judgebench, llm_judge_survey}, we re-annotated
all 5{,}000 anomalous images with Claude Sonnet 4.6 using a
cross-validation prompt that asks Claude to (i) independently classify
the anomaly without seeing the GPT-4o label, and (ii) rate the
GPT-4o-generated description on factuality and completeness. The
prompt and full output schema are in
Appendix~\ref{app:annotation_prompt}. Cost: \$$\sim$60 with prompt
caching enabled.

\paragraph{Inter-annotator agreement.}
Cross-LLM Cohen's $\kappa$ between GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet 4.6 on
the 11-class assignment is reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:disagreement_stats} along with raw agreement rates,
mean factuality scores, and the count of disagreements that were
manually reviewed. We follow the convention that
$\kappa > 0.6$ indicates substantial agreement. After human review
and adjudication of all disagreements, the resulting gold labels are
included in the released dataset.

\paragraph{Disagreement adjudication.}
For each image where GPT-4o and Claude assigned different classes
(or where Claude rated the GPT-4o description below 4 on factuality),
an author manually inspected the image and adjudicated the final
label. The full distribution of adjudication decisions (GPT-4o
correct / Claude correct / both wrong / ambiguous) is reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:disagreement_stats}.

\section{Compute Resources}
\label{app:compute}

All inference and analysis was performed on a single workstation with
two NVIDIA RTX 5090 GPUs (32 GB each). Total wall-clock time for the
full benchmark sweep:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item Binary task (15K images $\times$ 9 VLMs): $\sim$96 GPU-hours.
  \item Multiclass task (5K images $\times$ 9 VLMs): $\sim$32 GPU-hours.
  \item Description task (5K images $\times$ 9 VLMs): $\sim$45 GPU-hours.
  \item Multiclass consistency ($N=5$): $\sim$160 GPU-hours.
  \item Binary consistency ($N=5$): $\sim$480 GPU-hours.
  \item Hidden-state extraction for layer-PCA analysis: $\sim$24 GPU-hours.
  \item CLIP/SigLIP zero-shot baselines: $\sim$8 GPU-hours.
\end{itemize}
Total: $\sim$845 GPU-hours, plus $\sim$\$60 in Anthropic API spend
for cross-LLM annotation validation.
